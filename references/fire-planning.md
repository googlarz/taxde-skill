# FIRE Planning Guide

## What is FIRE?
Financial Independence, Retire Early. Accumulate enough wealth that investment returns cover your living expenses indefinitely.

## The Math
- **FIRE Number** = Annual Expenses ÷ Withdrawal Rate
- Standard withdrawal rate: 4% (based on Trinity Study / 30-year horizon)
- Conservative: 3.5% (longer retirement, more safety margin)
- Lean FIRE: 3% withdrawal, minimal lifestyle

## Example (Berlin-based)
Monthly expenses: €2,500 → Annual: €30,000
- FIRE at 4%: €750,000
- FIRE at 3.5%: €857,000

## Steps to FIRE

### 1. Calculate Your Number
- Track expenses for 3 months minimum
- Include all recurring costs
- Add 10-20% buffer for unexpected expenses

### 2. Maximize Savings Rate
- Target 50%+ savings rate for fastest path
- At 50% savings rate: ~17 years to FIRE
- At 30% savings rate: ~28 years to FIRE
- At 70% savings rate: ~8 years to FIRE

### 3. Invest Consistently
- Low-cost global index ETFs (VWCE, IWDA+EIMI)
- Regular monthly Sparplan (automated investment plan)
- Don't try to time the market

### 4. Optimize Taxes
- Use Freistellungsauftrag (€1,000 single / €2,000 married)
- Consider accumulating (thesaurierend) ETFs to defer capital gains
- Riester/Rürup if they fit your situation

## FIRE Variants
- **Fat FIRE**: Generous budget, typically €50,000+/year
- **Lean FIRE**: Minimal budget, €15,000-25,000/year
- **Barista FIRE**: Part-time work covers some expenses, smaller portfolio needed
- **Coast FIRE**: Enough invested that compounding will reach FIRE by traditional retirement age

## German-Specific Considerations
- GKV minimum contribution: ~€210/month if not employed (freelancer rate)
- KVdR: public health insurance in retirement is cheaper if you qualified for enough years
- Deutsche Rentenversicherung: don't forget your state pension entitlement
- Vorabpauschale: annual tax on accumulating fund gains (since 2018)
- Teilfreistellung: 30% of ETF gains are tax-free (for equity ETFs)
